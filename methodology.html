Research Design

This study employs six complementary econometric approaches:

  • Traditional Two-Period DiD for baseline estimates
  • Staggered DiD for multiple policy changes
  • Synthetic Control for counterfactual construction
  • Event Study for dynamic effects analysis
  • Triple Difference for additional robustness
  • RD with DiD for boundary analysis

Traditional Two-Period Difference-in-Differences

Following Card and Krueger (1994) methodology

The traditional difference-in-differences method compares changes in outcomes between treated and control states before and after minimum wage policy changes. This approach helps isolate the causal effect of minimum wage increases by controlling for both time-invariant differences between states and common time trends. By comparing the difference in outcomes between treatment and control groups over time, we can identify the impact of minimum wage policies while accounting for underlying trends and fixed characteristics of each state.

Model Specification

Y_ist = α + βTreat_s × Post_t + γ_s + δ_t + X_ist'θ + ε_ist

Where:

  • Y_ist: Outcome variable for individual i in state s at time t
  • Treat_s: Treatment indicator for state s
  • Post_t: Post-treatment period indicator
  • γ_s: State fixed effects
  • δ_t: Time fixed effects
  • X_ist: Vector of control variables

Identification Assumptions

  • Parallel Trends
    • Treatment and control states would follow parallel paths absent intervention
    • Verified through pre-treatment trend analysis
  • No Spillovers
    • Minimum wage changes in treated states do not affect control states
    • Tested through border county analysis
  • No Anticipation Effects
    • Economic actors do not adjust behavior before implementation
    • Examined through announcement effect analysis

Staggered Difference-in-Differences

Based on Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021)

The staggered difference-in-differences approach extends the traditional DiD method to handle multiple treatment periods and varying treatment timing across states. This method is particularly valuable for our analysis as different states implemented minimum wage changes at different times. It accounts for the dynamic nature of policy adoption and helps avoid bias that can arise when earlier treated units are used as controls for later treated units. The method provides a more nuanced understanding of treatment effects by explicitly modeling the timing of policy changes.

Extended Model

Y_ist = α + Σ_τ β_τ D_s(t-τ) + γ_s + δ_t + X_ist'θ + ε_ist

Where D_s(t-τ) indicates treatment status τ periods from initial treatment.

Term Explanations:

  • Σ_τ: Summation over all time periods relative to treatment
  • β_τ: Treatment effect τ periods away from initial treatment
  • D_s(t-τ): Indicator for being τ periods away from treatment in state s
  • γ_s: State fixed effects
  • δ_t: Time fixed effects
  • X_ist'θ: Control variables and their coefficients
  • ε_ist: Error term

Synthetic Control Method

Following Abadie et al. (2010)

The synthetic control method creates a data-driven comparison unit by combining multiple control states to match the pre-treatment characteristics of the treated state as closely as possible. This approach provides a more rigorous counterfactual by constructing a synthetic version of the treated state using weighted combinations of control states. The method is particularly useful when no single untreated state serves as an ideal comparison, allowing us to create a more credible control group for evaluating the impact of minimum wage policies.

Model Framework

Y_1t = δt + θtZ_1 + λtμ_1 + ε_1t

Term Explanations:

  • Y_1t: Outcome for treated unit at time t
  • δt: Unknown common factor
  • θt: Vector of unknown parameters
  • Z_1: Vector of observed covariates
  • λt: Unknown common factor loadings
  • μ_1: Vector of unobserved unit characteristics
  • ε_1t: Error term
W* = argmin (X_1 - X_0W)'V(X_1 - X_0W)

Term Explanations:

  • W*: Optimal weights for synthetic control
  • X_1: Vector of pre-intervention characteristics for treated unit
  • X_0: Matrix of pre-intervention characteristics for control units
  • V: Symmetric and positive semidefinite matrix
  • W: Vector of weights

Event Study Design

Based on Sun and Abraham (2021)

The event study design examines the dynamic effects of minimum wage changes by tracking outcomes before and after policy implementation. This approach allows us to identify both anticipatory effects and the evolution of policy impacts over time. By estimating treatment effects for multiple periods before and after the policy change, we can assess whether the impact of minimum wage increases varies over time and whether there are any pre-existing trends that might confound our analysis.

Dynamic Effects Model

Y_ist = α + Σ_k β_k D_ist^k + γ_i + δ_t + X_ist'θ + ε_ist

Where D_ist^k represents indicators for k periods relative to treatment.

Term Explanations:

  • Σ_k: Sum over all periods relative to event
  • β_k: Effect k periods relative to event
  • D_ist^k: Indicator for being k periods away from event
  • γ_i: Individual fixed effects
  • δ_t: Time fixed effects
  • X_ist'θ: Controls and their coefficients
  • ε_ist: Error term

Implementation Framework

  • Event Window: 24 months pre/post treatment
  • Multiple Events: Stacked regression approach
  • Heterogeneity Analysis: By wage increase size

Triple Difference (DDD)

Following Derenoncourt and Montialoux (2021)

The triple difference method adds an additional layer of comparison to the traditional DiD approach by incorporating a third dimension of variation, typically across different groups within treated and control states. In our analysis, we compare outcomes across age groups, education levels, and industry exposure. This method helps control for state-specific shocks that might affect all groups similarly and group-specific trends that might affect all states similarly, providing a more robust identification of the minimum wage effects on our target populations.

Model Specification

Y_isgt = α + βTreat_s × Post_t × Group_g + γ_st + δ_gt + η_sg + X_isgt'θ + ε_isgt

Term Explanations:

  • Y_isgt: Outcome for individual i in state s, group g, at time t
  • β: Triple-difference coefficient
  • Treat_s: Treatment state indicator
  • Post_t: Post-period indicator
  • Group_g: Group indicator (e.g., age group)
  • γ_st: State-by-time fixed effects
  • δ_gt: Group-by-time fixed effects
  • η_sg: State-by-group fixed effects
  • X_isgt'θ: Controls and their coefficients
  • ε_isgt: Error term

Additional Dimension

  • Age groups (16-19 vs. 25-64)
  • Education levels (HS vs. College)
  • Industry exposure

Regression Discontinuity with DiD

Based on Dell (2010)

The regression discontinuity with difference-in-differences approach combines the strengths of both methods to analyze minimum wage effects at policy thresholds. This method is particularly useful for examining effects at state borders where minimum wage policies change discontinuously. By comparing outcomes just above and below policy thresholds before and after implementation, we can identify local treatment effects while controlling for both spatial and temporal confounders. This approach is especially valuable for isolating the causal impact of minimum wage policies in geographic boundary regions.

Combined Approach

Y_ist = α + τ(D_s × Post_t) + β_1(X - c) + β_2(X - c)D_s + γ_s + δ_t + ε_ist

Term Explanations:

  • τ: Treatment effect at the discontinuity
  • D_s: Treatment indicator
  • X: Running variable
  • c: Cutoff value
  • β_1: Slope coefficient for control group
  • β_2: Differential slope for treatment group
  • γ_s: State fixed effects
  • δ_t: Time fixed effects
  • ε_ist: Error term
